# Provenance Guard — Planning

## 1. Detection Signals

Two independent signals, each producing a float in `[0, 1]` interpreted as
**P(content is AI-generated)**.

### Signal 1 — LLM judgment (Groq `llama-3.3-70b-versatile`)
- **What it measures:** Holistic semantic and stylistic coherence. The model
  is prompted to read the full passage and judge whether it reads as
  AI-generated, considering things like overly balanced "on one hand / on the
  other hand" framing, generic hedging, listicle-like structure, and
  topic/tone consistency.
- **Output shape:** JSON `{"ai_probability": float 0-1, "reasoning": str}`,
  requested at `temperature=0` for repeatability.
- **Why this signal:** LLMs are good at picking up on *meaning-level* tells
  (genericness, lack of a specific point of view, formulaic transitions) that
  a purely statistical method cannot see at all.
- **Blind spot:** It is a black box — we can't point to the exact textual
  evidence, it can be inconsistent across near-identical inputs, and a
  motivated AI user can trivially defeat it by asking the generator to
  "write casually, vary sentence length, use a personal anecdote."

### Signal 2 — Stylometric heuristics (pure Python, no ML)
- **What it measures:** Structural/statistical regularity of the writing —
  three sub-metrics combined:
  1. **Sentence-length coefficient of variation (CV = stdev/mean of words
     per sentence).** Low CV (very uniform sentence length) skews toward AI;
     human creative writing tends to have bursty, irregular sentence length.
  2. **Type-token ratio (unique words / total words).** Very low lexical
     diversity (heavy repetition of the same words) skews toward AI.
  3. **AI marker-phrase density** — occurrences per 100 words of stock
     phrases AI models overuse ("it is important to note", "in today's
     fast-paced world", "delve into", "navigate the complexities", "as an AI
     language model", "tapestry", "boasts", "underscores the importance",
     etc.).
- **Output shape:** single float 0-1, a weighted blend of the three
  normalized sub-metrics (45% uniformity, 30% marker density, 25% lexical
  diversity).
- **Why this signal:** It is fully deterministic, explainable per-feature,
  free, instant, and captures *structural* regularity — a fundamentally
  different property than the LLM's semantic judgment.
- **Blind spot:** Says nothing about meaning or content. A terse, formal
  human writer (technical writing, legal prose, a non-native English
  speaker) can score AI-like on uniformity alone. Easily defeated by
  prompting a generator for irregular sentence length.

### Combining into one confidence score
```
combined_score = 0.65 * llm_score + 0.35 * stylometric_score
```
LLM weighted higher because it is the stronger standalone signal in
practice; stylometrics get real weight specifically because they fail in
*different* cases than the LLM does, so the blend is more robust than either
alone — that's the point of requiring 2 distinct signals.

*(Note: these weights were tuned empirically in Milestone 4 — see README
"Spec Reflection" for how the first draft of 0.6/0.4 diverged from what
actually shipped.)*

## 2. Uncertainty Representation

`confidence` returned to the API caller **is** `combined_score` — a single
number meaning "estimated probability this is AI-generated." A 0.51 and a
0.95 are both "leans AI" but only one of them is *confidently* AI, and the
label thresholds below are designed so they produce visibly different text.

**Threshold design — deliberately asymmetric.** A false positive (calling a
human's work AI) does real reputational harm to a creator; a false negative
(missing AI-generated content) is comparatively low-stakes on a creative
platform. So the bar to declare "likely AI" is set higher than the bar to
declare "likely human," and the "uncertain" band in between is wide on
purpose:

| `combined_score` range | `attribution` | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| `>= 0.70` | `likely_ai` | Strong evidence required before accusing |
| `0.40 – 0.70` (exclusive bounds, 30-point band) | `uncertain` | Default when evidence is mixed or weak |
| `<= 0.40` | `likely_human` | Lower bar — we'd rather under-call AI than over-call it |

## 3. Transparency Label — exact text

All three variants interpolate the AI-likelihood as a whole-number percent
(`pct = round(combined_score * 100)`); wording itself is fixed.

| Attribution | Exact label text |
|---|---|
| `likely_ai` | `🤖 Likely AI-Generated — Our system estimates a {pct}% likelihood that this content was generated by AI, based on language-model analysis and writing-style patterns. If you wrote this yourself, you can appeal this classification.` |
| `likely_human` | `✍️ Likely Human-Written — Our system estimates a {pct}% likelihood that this content was written by a human, based on language-model analysis and writing-style patterns. No action is needed.` |
| `uncertain` | `❓ Uncertain Origin — Our system could not confidently determine whether this content was written by a human or AI (AI-likelihood: {pct}%). This reflects genuine uncertainty, not suspicion of wrongdoing.` |

## 4. Appeals Workflow
- **Who:** the creator (identified by `creator_id`) who owns the
  `content_id` being appealed. No auth system is built for this project — in
  production, this endpoint would require a session tied to the original
  `creator_id`.
- **What they provide:** `content_id` + free-text `creator_reasoning`
  explaining why they believe the classification is wrong.
- **What the system does:**
  1. Looks up the submission by `content_id` (404 if missing).
  2. Updates that row's `status` to `under_review`.
  3. Writes a new `audit_log` row with `event_type="appeal"`, carrying the
     original `attribution`/`confidence` alongside the new
     `appeal_reasoning`, so the audit trail shows the decision and the
     contest of that decision together.
  4. Returns a confirmation payload.
- **Human reviewer view:** `GET /log` (filtered mentally to
  `event_type="appeal"` or `status="under_review"`) shows every appeal next
  to the original signal scores that produced the disputed verdict — enough
  context to review without re-running detection. Automated re-classification
  is explicitly out of scope.

## 5. Anticipated Edge Cases
1. **Very short submissions** (a haiku, a 2-sentence flash-fiction
   fragment). Sentence-length CV and type-token ratio are statistically
   meaningless with only 1-2 sentences — the stylometric signal degrades
   toward a noisy ~0.5 and the system ends up leaning almost entirely on the
   LLM signal, which itself is also less reliable on very short text.
2. **Formal human writing** (academic abstracts, legal-style prose,
   non-native-English-speaker writing). These naturally have low sentence
   length variance and sometimes overlap with the AI marker-phrase list
   ("it is important to note" is just... formal English), pushing the
   stylometric score toward "AI-like" even though a human wrote it. This is
   exactly the asymmetric-threshold scenario the design tries to protect
   against — it should land in `uncertain`, not `likely_ai`.

## Architecture

### Submission flow
```
            POST /submit {text, creator_id}
                       |
                       v
        +-------------------------------+
        |  Flask route: submit()        |
        |  - generate content_id (uuid) |
        +-------------------------------+
                       |
        raw text passed to BOTH signals in parallel (sequentially in code)
                       |
        +--------------+----------------+
        |                               |
        v                               v
+------------------+          +-----------------------+
| Signal 1: Groq    |          | Signal 2: Stylometric |
| LLM judgment       |          | heuristics (pure py)  |
| -> llm_score 0-1   |          | -> stylometric_score  |
+------------------+          +-----------------------+
        |                               |
        +--------------+----------------+
                       v
        combined_score = 0.65*llm + 0.35*stylometric
                       v
        +-------------------------------+
        |  threshold -> attribution      |
        |  (likely_ai / uncertain /      |
        |   likely_human)                |
        +-------------------------------+
                       v
        +-------------------------------+
        |  label = render_label(         |
        |    attribution, combined_score)|
        +-------------------------------+
                       v
        +-------------------------------+
        |  persist submission row +      |
        |  write audit_log row           |
        |  (SQLite)                      |
        +-------------------------------+
                       v
        JSON response: {content_id, attribution,
          confidence, label, signals, status}
```

### Appeal flow
```
   POST /appeal {content_id, creator_reasoning}
                  |
                  v
   +--------------------------------+
   | Flask route: appeal()           |
   | - look up submission by id      |
   |   (404 if not found)            |
   +--------------------------------+
                  |
                  v
   +--------------------------------+
   | UPDATE submissions               |
   |   SET status = 'under_review'    |
   +--------------------------------+
                  |
                  v
   +--------------------------------+
   | INSERT audit_log row             |
   |   event_type='appeal',           |
   |   carries original attribution/  |
   |   confidence + appeal_reasoning  |
   +--------------------------------+
                  |
                  v
   JSON response: {content_id, status: "under_review", message}
```

Both flows write to the **same** `audit_log` table, so `GET /log` shows
classification and appeal events interleaved in chronological order — that's
the single canonical record graders/reviewers look at.

- **M3 (submission endpoint + first signal):** Fed the AI tool the
  "Detection Signals" section above (Signal 1 description) + the submission
  flow diagram. Asked for: Flask app skeleton with `POST /submit` stub, and
  the `llm_signal.py` function matching the documented `{ai_probability,
  reasoning}` JSON contract. Verified by calling the signal function directly
  against 2-3 hand-picked strings before wiring it into the route, and
  printing the raw Groq response to confirm the JSON contract actually holds
  (LLMs occasionally wrap JSON in prose — added a regex/fallback strip for
  that).
- **M4 (second signal + confidence scoring):** Fed the "Detection Signals"
  + "Uncertainty Representation" sections + diagram. Asked for: the
  `stylometric_signal.py` function (3 sub-metrics) and `scoring.py`
  implementing the documented blend and threshold table. Verified by running
  the M4 test-suite inputs (clearly AI, clearly human, two borderline) and
  checking whether the resulting `attribution` values matched intuition —
  they didn't at the originally-specified `0.6/0.4` weights and `0.80/0.35`
  thresholds (the obviously-AI sample landed in `uncertain`, not
  `likely_ai`), so the weights/thresholds were recalibrated to `0.65/0.35`
  and `0.70/0.40` and re-verified against all four inputs before moving on.
- **M5 (production layer):** Fed the "Transparency Label" + "Appeals
  Workflow" sections + appeal diagram. Asked for: `labels.py` render
  function and the `POST /appeal` route. Verified by calling
  `render_label()` directly with scores `0.95`, `0.5`, `0.1` and diffing
  against the exact strings in section 3 (caught one mismatch: the
  generated code used `>` instead of `>=` at the `0.80` boundary, fixed
  before integrating), then round-tripped a real `content_id` through
  `/submit` → `/appeal` → `/log` to confirm `status` flips to
  `under_review` and the appeal row appears.
